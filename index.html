Developmental Psychopathology

HDF 342 · UT Austin

Human Development & Family Sciences

This course reshaped how I think about health communication. Understanding how development goes awry — through attachment disruption, trauma, and neurodevelopmental difference — gives me the mechanistic foundation to design interventions that meet people where they actually are, not where we assume them to be.

Course Rationale

Why a communication researcher studies psychopathology

Effective health messaging requires more than persuasion theory. It demands an understanding of the psychological substrate — the developmental processes, attachment patterns, and neurobiological mechanisms that shape how people receive and respond to information about their own well-being.

This course bridges two fields that rarely speak directly to each other: developmental science and communication strategy. The goal isn't to diagnose — it's to understand the conditions under which messages fail, and to design around those conditions with greater precision.

Core Concepts

Frameworks that changed my thinking

01

Transactional Development

Child ↔ environment as reciprocal system
+

Sameroff's transactional model reframes development as a continuous, bidirectional exchange between the individual and their context. A child's behavior shapes caregiver responses, which in turn shape the child's subsequent development.

Communication insight: Health messages don't land in a vacuum. They interact with existing belief systems, relational dynamics, and life history. Effective intervention design must account for this feedback loop.

Sameroff, 2009
02

Attachment Theory

Internal working models of trust and help-seeking
+

Bowlby and Ainsworth's attachment framework shows how early relational experiences create internal working models — cognitive-affective templates that shape how people seek help, trust information sources, and respond to vulnerability.

Communication insight: Insecure attachment patterns may predict resistance to health messages that require vulnerability (e.g., mental health screening, therapy referrals). Understanding attachment style helps tailor message framing.

Bowlby, 1969 Ainsworth, 1978
03

Neurodevelopmental Disorders

ASD, ADHD, and information processing differences
+

Studying ASD and ADHD as neurodevelopmental variation (not simply deficit) reveals how differences in sensory processing, executive function, and social cognition affect how health information is received and acted upon.

Communication insight: "One-size-fits-all" health messaging systematically excludes neurodivergent populations. Accessible communication design must account for processing differences — not just language barriers.

Inclusive Design
04

Resilience & Protective Factors

What enables thriving despite adversity
+

Masten's "ordinary magic" framework identifies resilience not as an exceptional trait but as the product of fundamental adaptive systems — attachment relationships, self-regulation, agency, and community support.

Communication insight: Interventions framed around deficit ("fix what's broken") consistently underperform those framed around growth and existing strengths. This aligns directly with Yeager's wise intervention principles.

Masten, 2001 Yeager & Walton

Research Application

How this shapes my thesis work

My research examines self-silencing and help-seeking among Korean American midlife women. This course gave me the developmental vocabulary to understand why these patterns exist — not just that they exist.

Self-Silencing as Attachment Strategy

Korean cultural norms around emotional restraint (참다, chamda) may function as learned attachment behavior — suppressing needs to maintain relational harmony. This is not simply "cultural difference" but a developmentally embedded communication pattern.

Help-Seeking as Vulnerability Exposure

Seeking professional help requires tolerating vulnerability — a capacity shaped by attachment history. For women whose internal working models discourage trust in external support, the barrier is developmental, not informational.

Intervention Design Implications

Messages that acknowledge the relational cost of help-seeking (rather than simply promoting "awareness") may be more effective. The developmental lens suggests meeting people at their attachment style, not at our assumptions about rational decision-making.

Health Equity Through Developmental Justice

When we ignore developmental context in health communication, we reproduce inequity. Culturally informed intervention design must integrate both the sociocultural and the developmental — they are not separate systems.

The question is not whether development shapes communication — it clearly does. The question is whether we, as communicators, are willing to design with that complexity rather than around it.

— Reflection, Fall 2025
