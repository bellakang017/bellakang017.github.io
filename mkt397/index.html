Empirics-First Methodology & Causal Inference

This Week's Reading

  • ◇
    Rubin (1974)— Potential outcomes framework; counterfactual reasoning
  • ◇
    Du, R. (Lecture Notes)— Empirics-first approach: start with data patterns, then build theory
↓

My Interpretation

What the empirics-first lens changes

Prof. Du's methodology inverts the typical research flow. Instead of "theory → hypothesis → test," we begin with empirical regularitiesPatterns consistently observed in data before any theoretical explanation is offered. The starting point in empirics-first research. — patterns in real data — and let them guide theoretical development.

This resonates deeply with my health communication work. In studying Korean American women's help-seeking behavior, the patterns (low utilization despite high need) preceded my theoretical framework. The data spoke first.

The Rubin Causal ModelFramework where causal effects are defined as the difference between potential outcomes under treatment vs. control. The "fundamental problem" is that we can only observe one outcome per unit. formalizes something health communicators often struggle with: we can't observe the counterfactual. What would have happened to message recipients if they hadn't received the intervention?

Research Gap / Emerging Question

Where this connects to health communication

Most health communication studies rely on self-report measuresData collected by asking participants to report their own behaviors, attitudes, or experiences. Subject to recall bias and social desirability bias. with limited causal identification. What if we applied the empirics-first methodology to health message evaluation — starting with behavioral data (search patterns, clinic visits, prescription fills) rather than attitudinal surveys?

Emerging question: Can we identify natural experimentsSituations where treatment assignment occurs "as if" randomly due to external circumstances (policy changes, geographic boundaries, timing variations), enabling causal inference without a designed experiment. in health communication contexts — policy changes, media events, cultural shifts — that allow causal identification of message effects on behavior?

Visual Scaffold — Causal Inference Logic
Causal Effect = E[Y₁ᵢ] − E[Y₀ᵢ]

where Y₁ᵢ = outcome if unit i receives treatment
      Y₀ᵢ = outcome if unit i does not

Problem: We observe Y₁ᵢ OR Y₀ᵢ, never both.
Solution: Identify a credible comparison group.
The fundamental problem of causal inference — we can never observe the counterfactual for the same individual.

Week 1 Takeaway: "Empirics first" isn't anti-theory — it's a commitment to letting data patterns discipline our theorizing. For health communication research, this means starting with observed behavioral gaps (who isn't seeking help, and when?) before constructing explanations.

Doing Research with Surveys

February 2026
한국어 Study Guide →

This Week's Reading

Survey Design & Construct Measurement

  • ◇
    Vomberg & Klarmann (2021)— "Crafting Survey Research": systematic process for conducting survey research — from questionnaire design to data quality control
  • ◇
    Hohenberg & Taylor (2020)— "Measuring Customer Satisfaction and Customer Loyalty": operationalization, scales, and measurement systems for two key marketing constructs
↓
2
My Interpretation

The measurement chain: tool → construct → decision

These two papers form a logical pair. Vomberg & Klarmann teach you how to build the instrument — the survey itself. Hohenberg & Taylor show you what to build it for — measuring satisfaction and loyalty, two of the most consequential (and most error-prone) constructs in marketing.

The deeper lesson is about cascading measurement error. If your survey is poorly designed (biased wording, bad sampling, common method variance), your satisfaction scores will be distorted. If satisfaction is distorted, your loyalty predictions will be wrong. If loyalty predictions are wrong, the firm allocates resources to the wrong customers. Measurement error doesn't stay contained — it propagates through the entire CRM-Outcome ChainFramework: Marketing Activities → Customer Attitudes (satisfaction, trust) + Factual Reasons (switching costs, contracts) → Loyalty Intentions → Loyalty Behavior → Economic Outcomes. Adapted from Kumar & Reinartz (2012)..

Reliability vs. Validity. ReliabilityConsistency of measurement. If you ask 4 similar questions and answers move together, the scale is reliable. Cronbach's Alpha (α ≥ 0.7) and Composite Reliability (CR ≥ 0.7) are standard metrics. is necessary but not sufficient. A scale can be perfectly consistent while measuring the wrong thing. Hohenberg & Taylor's Table 1 reports psychometric properties — CA, CR, AVEAverage Variance Extracted. ≥ 0.5 means the construct explains more variance in its items than measurement error does. Below 0.5, noise dominates signal. — for each satisfaction scale, giving concrete quality benchmarks.

Common Method Bias. When you measure both the independent variable (satisfaction) and the dependent variable (loyalty intention) in the same survey, respondents unconsciously align their answers. The observed correlation is inflated — not because the relationship is strong, but because of the measurement methodCommon Method Bias (CMB): artificial inflation of relationships when IVs and DVs share the same measurement method — same survey, same time point, same respondent. Harman's single-factor test and marker variable techniques are common diagnostics.. This is precisely why Hohenberg & Taylor recommend measuring loyalty behavior through objective CRM data rather than self-report.

Intentions ≠ Behavior. Sheppard et al. (1988) showed that stated behavioral intentions are often a weak predictor of actual behavior. Customers who say they'll repurchase often don't. This is why the chapter advocates a dual measurement approach — subjective surveys for intentions, objective databases for behavior.

The Satisfaction–Loyalty Disconnect. A meta-analysis found that satisfaction explains less than 25% of loyalty variance (Szymanski & Henard 2001). Over 60% of satisfied customers still defect (Reichheld 1996). Other drivers — trust, commitment, switching costsCosts (financial, psychological, procedural) that customers incur when changing providers. Includes contractual obligations, learning costs, and loss of accumulated benefits (e.g., loyalty points)., contractual obligations — matter enormously. This challenges any system that treats satisfaction as the sole predictor.

Net Promoter Score. NPS reduces loyalty to a single question: "On 0–10, would you recommend?" Promoters (9–10) minus Detractors (0–6) = NPS. Its appeal is efficiency. But Keiningham et al. (2007) found no support for the claim that NPS is the single most reliable growth indicator. The chapter recommends integrating NPS as one component — not the whole system.

Self-Selection & Heckman. When measuring loyalty through loyalty programs, customers self-selectSelf-selection bias: when individuals choose to participate in a program based on characteristics correlated with the outcome of interest. Loyalty program members may spend more not because of the program, but because big spenders are more likely to enroll. into programs. Comparing members vs. non-members confounds program effects with pre-existing differences. The Heckman two-stage correction addresses this by first estimating enrollment probability (Probit), then including the inverse Mills ratio in the outcome equation to purge selection bias.

3
Research Gap / Emerging Question

What if we applied these measurement principles to health communication?

Most health communication interventions rely on self-reported attitude change measured in post-intervention surveys. But this week's readings expose how fragile that approach is: common method bias inflates treatment effects, intentions don't predict behavior, and satisfaction with a health message doesn't guarantee adherence.

What if we adopted Hohenberg & Taylor's dual measurement framework in health contexts? Measure message reception and attitudes through carefully designed surveys (applying Vomberg & Klarmann's principles), but measure actual health behavior through objective data — EHR records, pharmacy fills, app-tracked exercise, clinic visit logs.

The Confirmation-Disconfirmation ParadigmOliver (1980): Satisfaction = Perceived Performance (P) vs. Expected Performance (E). P > E → satisfaction. P < E → dissatisfaction. Satisfaction is about the gap between expectations and experience, not absolute quality. also has untapped potential here. Health message effectiveness may depend not on absolute message quality, but on the gap between what patients expected from the health system and what they experienced. A message that sets appropriate expectations might produce more durable behavior change than one that maximizes initial satisfaction.

Emerging question: Can we design health communication measurement systems that combine survey-based attitude assessment with objective behavioral data — and use the Heckman correctionTwo-stage method: Stage 1 (Probit) estimates participation probability → computes inverse Mills ratio λ(z) = ϕ(z)/Φ(z). Stage 2 (OLS) includes λ as a regressor. If ρ is significant, selection bias was present. to address self-selection into health programs (wellness apps, patient portals, support groups) the same way marketing researchers address self-selection into loyalty programs?

Visual Scaffold — Confirmation-Disconfirmation Paradigm
Oliver (1980) — Satisfaction Formation

Perceived Performance (P) vs. Expected Performance (E)

P > E → Positive Disconfirmation → Satisfaction
P = E → Confirmation → Satisfaction
P < E → Negative Disconfirmation → Dissatisfaction
Satisfaction is not about absolute quality — it's about the gap between what was expected and what was experienced.
Visual Scaffold — Heckman Two-Stage Correction
Stage 1 (Selection): y₁* = X₁β₁ + e₁ → Probit
Stage 2 (Outcome): E[y₂ | y₁* > 0] = X₂β₂ + ρλ(X₁β₁)

λ(z) = ϕ(z) / Φ(z) — inverse Mills ratio

If ρ significant → selection bias exists → correction needed
If ρ ≈ 0 → no selection bias → simpler model suffices
The inverse Mills ratio captures the conditional probability of being observed (e.g., enrolled in a loyalty program) and purges its influence from the outcome equation.

Week 7 Takeaway: "What you measure shapes what you manage." Satisfaction surveys feel rigorous, but without validity checks, CMB controls, and behavioral data triangulation, they can create a measurement mirage — numbers that feel precise but point in the wrong direction. For both marketing and health communication, the path forward requires measuring constructs at multiple levels (overall + detailed), through multiple methods (survey + objective), while being honest about what surveys can and cannot capture.
